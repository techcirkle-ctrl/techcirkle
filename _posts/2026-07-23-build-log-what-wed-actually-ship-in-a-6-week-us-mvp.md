## Week by week: what actually ships

Here is the concrete six-week arc, the way it runs in practice:

- **Week 1** - intensive scoping and a clickable prototype. No production code yet, just alignment on the one workflow that matters.
- **Weeks 2 to 4** - the accelerated core build. AI-scaffolded features with automated tests running in parallel, not bolted on afterward.
- **Week 5** - integration, the one or two AI capabilities that differentiate the product, and analytics instrumentation from the first release.
- **Week 6** - hardening, a private beta, and demo preparation.

Six weeks would have been implausible for a credible US-built MVP a few years ago. It is achievable now because the low-value engineering work is automated and the senior team spends its time on scope and architecture judgment. The schedule compresses because the busywork disappears, not because quality does. If your idea is more ambitious than a six-week scope allows, that is a signal to narrow the MVP, not extend it.

## What we deliberately fake or skip

Scope discipline is the single biggest determinant of cost and speed, and it is where founders most often sabotage themselves. The art is deciding what to build for real, what to fake convincingly, and what to leave out:

- **Build for real:** the one workflow that proves your value hypothesis, plus the analytics to measure whether users complete it.
- **Fake convincingly:** onboarding, admin panels, and back-office tooling can be manual at first - the concierge MVP pattern.
- **Shortcut with AI:** search, categorization, summarization, and onboarding can lean on a model instead of a hand-built feature.
- **Leave out entirely:** settings pages, edge-case flows, multi-tier permissions, anything a design partner has not explicitly asked for.

A bloated MVP is the most common reason US startups run out of runway before they learn anything.

## The trap in the log: a prototype that can't scale

The dark side of fast, cheap, AI-assisted MVPs is the rewrite. Founders arrive having validated an idea with a first version that now cannot be extended - the data model is wrong, there are no tests, security was an afterthought, and the AI-generated code nobody fully understands has become a liability. Validation succeeded, but the codebase has to be thrown away, exactly when momentum matters most.

Avoiding this is not over-engineering - that is the opposite mistake. It is a small number of decisions made right the first time: a data model that anticipates the obvious next features, automated tests around the core workflow, real authentication rather than a shortcut, and infrastructure that scales rather than gets rebuilt. An experienced team builds these in without inflating the timeline because it knows which corners are safe to cut. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much do MVP development services in USA cost in 2026?

A US-built MVP typically ranges from about $15,000 for a lean validation build with one core feature to $180,000 for an ambitious or regulated product. Most standard startup MVPs with auth, payments, analytics, and an AI feature or two land between $40,000 and $90,000. Scope discipline moves this number more than anything else.

### Can a real MVP actually ship in six weeks?

Yes, for a standard scope on an AI-augmented team: one week of scoping and prototyping, roughly three weeks of accelerated core build with automated testing in parallel, then integration, differentiation, and hardening. The timeline collapsed from the old four-to-six-month norm because AI automates the low-value engineering work, not because quality is cut.

### Is the sub-$40k MVP tier real, or a stripped-down demo?

It is real, but it is deliberately narrow. AI opened a genuine sub-$40,000 lane for validation-grade MVPs built to a launch-ready standard with one core feature and a minimal backend. It is not a throwaway mockup - it is the smallest real thing that produces signal. Anything with heavy integrations, compliance scope, or multiple AI capabilities moves up into the higher tiers.

### What should I build for real versus fake in an MVP?

Build for real only the one workflow that proves your core value hypothesis, plus the analytics to measure it. Fake or manually run onboarding, admin, and back-office tooling using the concierge pattern. Lean on AI for search and categorization. Leave out settings pages, edge cases, and multi-tier permissions entirely.

### How do I stop my MVP from becoming a rewrite after it succeeds?

Insist on a few foundations done right: a data model that anticipates obvious next features, automated tests around the core workflow, real authentication, and scalable infrastructure. This is not over-engineering - it avoids the throw-away rewrite that costs months exactly when your validated product needs momentum. An experienced partner builds these in without inflating the timeline.

### Should I hire a US MVP team or go offshore to save money?

For MVP work specifically, iteration speed usually beats a lower hourly rate. An MVP is a fast learning loop where requirements change weekly, and that loop breaks across a large time-zone gap. A US-based or US-led hybrid team turns a Tuesday user interview into a Thursday product change, which is worth more during validation than the offshore saving.

### Will I own the MVP code and IP?

You should own all of it, including intellectual property, in writing from the first commit. Confirm it explicitly in the contract. If a provider is evasive about IP or wants to retain rights to reuse your MVP's code, treat it as a serious red flag and choose a different partner.
